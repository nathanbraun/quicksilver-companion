Irish-born natural philosopher (1627-1691), often called the father of modern chemistry.

## Life and Work

Boyle was the fourteenth child of the Earl of Cork, one of the richest men in the British Isles. He never needed to work for a living, and he used that freedom to do science full time. He was deeply religious and saw experimental investigation as a way of understanding God's creation.

His most famous result is [Boyle's Law](/topic/boyles-law) — the inverse relationship between the pressure and volume of a gas. He arrived at it through meticulous air pump experiments conducted with [Robert Hooke](/topic/robert-hooke), who built the pumps. These experiments were landmark demonstrations of the new experimental method: instead of arguing from first principles, you built apparatus and measured things.

Boyle attacked the Aristotelian four-element theory (earth, air, fire, water) in *The Sceptical Chymist* (1661), arguing that elements should be defined by experiment, not philosophy. He was a founding Fellow of the [Royal Society](/topic/royal-society).

## In the Novel

Daniel works in Boyle's laboratory, and Boyle is part of the circle of natural philosophers Daniel moves through in London. His wealth and piety make him a contrast to the scrappier, more contentious [Hooke](/topic/robert-hooke).
