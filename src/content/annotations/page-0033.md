Enoch leaves instructions for Newton's education.

**"Show the boy Euclid and let him find his own way"** — Enoch tells Clarke to have his brother give Newton Euclid's *Elements*. This is historically grounded: the real Newton encountered Euclid at Cambridge and famously found it "trivially easy" — he skipped ahead to the hard parts. Stephenson moves this discovery earlier. Euclid's *Elements* (c. 300 BC) is one of the most influential books ever written — the foundation of geometric reasoning for two millennia. The instruction "let him find his own way" captures something essential about Newton's mind: you couldn't teach him in the conventional sense, only point him at problems and get out of the way.

**The Gödel connection** — Daniel later develops an indexing system using products of prime numbers, which one wiki contributor recognized as suspiciously similar to Gödel numbering — the technique Kurt Gödel used in his 1931 Incompleteness Theorem. Stephenson is quietly threading computational thinking back through history, connecting Newton's era to the 20th-century logic that would eventually produce computers. It also connects to [Wilkins](/topic/john-wilkins)'s dream of a universal philosophical language — another attempt to encode all knowledge in a formal system.
