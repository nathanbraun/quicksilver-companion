Enoch recalls the execution of [Charles I](/topic/charles-i), which he apparently witnessed — one of the first hints that his lifespan is impossible.

**Charing Cross** — Stephenson's own annotation: the beheading took place on the north side of the Banqueting House, facing Charing Cross. In the 1640s, "Charing Cross" meant a broader area than today — it was the only spot from which an ordinary person could have glimpsed the execution. Enoch saying he was "at Charing Cross" is "a little bit imprecise, but within the normal limits of imprecision."

**The Restoration** — The return of the monarchy in 1660 under [Charles II](/topic/charles-ii), eldest son of the beheaded king. Between them: the [English Civil War](/topic/english-civil-war), [Cromwell](/topic/oliver-cromwell)'s Puritan republic, and a decade that reshaped everything. Daniel, [Newton](/topic/isaac-newton), and the [Royal Society](/topic/royal-society) generation grew up in that republic and came of age in Charles II's libertine court. The whipsaw between those two worlds — Puritan seriousness vs. Restoration excess — runs through the entire book.

**"A loud fellow" — Archbishop William Laud** — Charles I's chief religious enforcer. Laud used the Star Chamber (a royal court with no jury and no appeal) to crush Puritan dissent. [Drake Waterhouse](/topic/drake-waterhouse) (Daniel's father) lost his ears and nose to Laud's courts — a real punishment inflicted on real Puritan pamphleteers like William Prynne. Laud was executed in 1645. For families like the Waterhouses, the entire Civil War was vindication.
