Young Ben Franklin defends Dr. Waterhouse, earning an unvoiced "Oh dear" from the narrator.

**"Oh dear"** — The interjection comes from outside the scene, breaking the fourth wall slightly. The 1713 frame chapters use a close-to-omniscient narrator; the Book 2 chapters read as picaresque adventure (Jack's voice); the [Eliza](/topic/eliza) chapters are epistolary — letters with hidden ciphers the reader can't decode. Stephenson is running three different novelistic modes simultaneously, a structure that mirrors [Leibniz](/topic/gottfried-wilhelm-von-leibniz)'s idea that the same reality can be described from multiple perspectives.

**Ben's precocity** — Franklin at seven, already arguing with adults and defending an old man he's just met. The real Franklin was largely self-educated, reading everything he could get his hands on from childhood. By twelve he was apprenticed to his brother's print shop. Stephenson catches the boy at the last moment before his life's trajectory becomes obvious.
