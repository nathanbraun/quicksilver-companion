Enoch continues his walking history lesson for Ben and Godfrey, covering Cromwell, the Dutch, and the roots of England's political factions.

**"Killed a great deal of Irishmen"** — Ben's summary of [Cromwell](/topic/english-civil-war). Accurate. Cromwell's 1649-1653 conquest of Ireland killed an estimated 15-40% of the population through combat, famine, and plague. Massacres at Drogheda and Wexford. Catholic land confiscated wholesale. The scars lasted centuries.

**Navigation Act** — Cromwell's 1651 act required goods to be shipped on English vessels, crippling Dutch trade dominance. The Dutch Republic was the 17th century's economic superpower — tiny but rich, with the world's largest merchant fleet, the first stock exchange, and the first multinational corporation (the VOC). England wanted what they had. The Act triggered the First Anglo-Dutch War (1652-1654), the first of three naval wars between the two Protestant trading powers. For the novel, this matters enormously: it's the origin of England as a naval-commercial empire, and the Dutch financial innovations [Eliza](/topic/eliza) discovers in Book 2 are what England is busy copying.

**"Granted all men — even Jews — the right to worship"** — Cromwell readmitted Jews to England in 1656, three centuries after Edward I expelled them. His motive was partly religious (millenarian Puritans believed Jewish return to England was a precondition for the Second Coming) and partly commercial (Sephardic Jews expelled from Spain had built powerful trade networks through Amsterdam).

**[John Comstock](/topic/john-comstock) and [Thomas More Anglesey](/topic/thomas-more-anglesey)** — Stephenson's own annotation: both are fictional. "Don't bother Googling them." Comstock borrows from John Evelyn ([Royal Society](/topic/royal-society) diarist) and the Earl of Clarendon. Anglesey "stands in for a lot of Francophilic crypto-Catholic courtiers who were thick on the ground in those days."

**Francis Bacon, Galileo, Descartes** — Enoch's list of "light-bringers." Bacon (1561-1626) invented the empirical method — test ideas against observation, not authority. Galileo (1564-1642) turned the telescope on the heavens and proved Copernicus right, earning house arrest from the Inquisition. Descartes (1596-1650) argued mathematics could describe all of nature. These three made the Scientific Revolution possible, and the [Royal Society](/topic/royal-society) is their direct heir.
