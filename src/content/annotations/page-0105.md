Daniel and Isaac witness the grim physical reality of plague-era London.

**"Grown from the corpses of rats"** — The original wiki notes the folk belief that certain growths sprang spontaneously from dead animals. Spontaneous generation — the idea that living organisms could arise from non-living matter — was still widely accepted in the 1660s. Francesco Redi's experiments disproving it for insects were published only in 1668, and the theory wasn't fully killed until Pasteur's work in the 1860s.

**"Rain of ticks"** — The [plague](/topic/plague) was transmitted by fleas on rats, but nobody in 1665 understood this. The flea vector wasn't identified until 1898 by Paul-Louis Simond. In the absence of germ theory, Londoners blamed miasma (bad air), divine punishment, astrological conjunctions, and — as here — parasites raining from the sky. The desperation for explanation was rational in its way: the disease killed so fast (often within days of the first symptoms) that any cause seemed plausible.
