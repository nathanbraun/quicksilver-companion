Daniel lists the dangers of crossing the Atlantic.

**"Seasickness, pirates, scurvy, mass drownings"** — Daniel, as a Fellow of the [Royal Society](/topic/royal-society), knows the odds better than most. Each item on his list was a genuine killer:

**Scurvy** — The great maritime plague. Caused by vitamin C deficiency, it killed an estimated two million sailors between 1500 and 1800 — more than combat, storms, and all other diseases combined. Symptoms appeared after about six weeks without fresh food: fatigue, bleeding gums, reopening of old wounds, then death. The cure (citrus fruit) had been observed repeatedly — a Royal Navy surgeon named James Lancaster demonstrated it in 1601 — but the knowledge kept getting lost because no one understood *why* it worked. The Navy wouldn't officially mandate lemon juice until 1795, almost a century after Daniel's voyage.

**[Pirates](/topic/pirates)** — Daniel's timing is terrible. The [Treaty of Utrecht](/topic/treaty-of-utrecht) (1713) had just ended the War of the Spanish Succession, releasing thousands of trained sailors and licensed privateers into unemployment. Many turned pirate. The period 1713-1726 is literally the Golden Age of Piracy — [Blackbeard](/topic/blackbeard), Bartholomew Roberts, "Calico Jack" Rackham. The *Minerva*'s pirate-hating Captain van Hoek has good reason to be vigilant.

**Mass drownings** — Navigation in 1713 was imprecise enough that entire fleets could miss their targets. [Longitude](/topic/longitude) was still unsolvable at sea. In 1707, just six years before this chapter, four Royal Navy ships struck the Scilly Isles in fog and sank, killing nearly 2,000 sailors — one of the worst maritime disasters in British history, and a direct spur to Parliament's Longitude Act of 1714.

**Daniel's reluctance** — He knows all of this and says so. That he eventually boards the *Minerva* anyway — at age 67, in October (storm season) — tells you how seriously he takes [Princess Caroline](/topic/caroline-of-ansbach)'s summons. Or how much he fears saying no to Enoch Root.
