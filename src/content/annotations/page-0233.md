Medical practitioners of the 17th century and their archaic titles.

**"Chirurgeon"** — As you might guess from attempting to pronounce it, this is an archaic form of "surgeon." The word comes from the Greek *cheirourgos* (hand-worker), via Old French *cirurgien*. In the 17th century, surgery was considered a lower trade than medicine proper — surgeons were grouped with barbers in the Worshipful Company of Barbers and Surgeons (not separated until 1745). A physician diagnosed and prescribed; a chirurgeon cut, stitched, and amputated. The distinction mattered socially: physicians were university-educated gentlemen, while surgeons learned by apprenticeship.
