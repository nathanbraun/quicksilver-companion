The geography of aristocratic London emerges through the Comstock family's residence.

**"Comstock House"** — Stephenson's annotation: "There were at the time at least three great family compounds situated along Piccadilly near its intersection with St. James's Street. One of these belonged to the Earl of Clarendon, who is one of several contemporary figures from whom [John Comstock](/topic/john-comstock) is freely adapted." The only one that survives today is Burlington House, now home to the Royal Academy. Piccadilly in the 1670s was the western edge of [London's](/topic/restoration-london) built-up area — a row of grand mansions with gardens stretching north into open countryside. The Comstock compound represents the wealth and political power of the Restoration aristocracy, the world Daniel must navigate as he moves between the [Royal Society](/topic/royal-society)'s intellectual circles and the court's political ones.
