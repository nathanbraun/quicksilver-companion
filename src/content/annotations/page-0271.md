Daniel sees Leibniz's mechanical calculator for the first time — gears, cranks, and the dream of mechanized thought.

**"Daniel saw gears, cranks, shafts"** — [Leibniz](/topic/gottfried-wilhelm-von-leibniz) brought a prototype of his [calculating machine](/topic/computing-machines) to London in January 1673 to demonstrate to the [Royal Society](/topic/royal-society). The machine could add, subtract, multiply, divide, and extract square roots using a system of stepped drums (cylinders with teeth of varying lengths). It was a significant advance over Pascal's Pascaline of 1642, which could only add and subtract. Leibniz was so proud of the invention he once considered a commemorative medal bearing the motto *SUPERIOR TO MAN*. He later had a version made for Peter the Great to send to the Emperor of China. But the machine was never fully reliable — the carrying mechanism couldn't handle all cases automatically. The real significance was the idea behind it: that reasoning itself could be mechanized. As the original wiki notes, "and you thought Neal was making this up about Leibniz."
