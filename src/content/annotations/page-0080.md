Newton's first encounter with real mathematics happens by accident.

**Stourbridge Fair** — In 1661, Newton strolled to Stourbridge Fair near Cambridge and picked up a book on astrology. He couldn't understand it because of the geometry and trigonometry. So he bought a copy of Euclid's *Elements* — found it too obvious — then moved on to Descartes' *Géométrie*, which was not obvious at all. This accidental sequence launched Newton's mathematical education. Stourbridge Fair was one of the largest trade fairs in Europe, held annually on Stourbridge Common outside Cambridge. It sold everything from wool and hops to books, spices, and (as the wiki's contributors note with amusement) the services of "speciality prostitutes." For Newton, it provided something more consequential than any of these: a doorway into mathematics that his formal Cambridge education had entirely failed to open.
