The calculus priority dispute is laid out — the reason for Daniel's journey.

**"The [priority dispute](/topic/calculus-priority-dispute) has turned vicious"** — [Newton](/topic/isaac-newton) vs. [Leibniz](/topic/gottfried-wilhelm-von-leibniz) over who invented calculus. Both developed it independently in the 1660s-70s. Newton got there first but kept it secret; Leibniz published first and created the notation we still use (dy/dx, the integral sign ∫). By 1712, Newton had secretly authored the [Royal Society](/topic/royal-society)'s supposedly impartial investigation, which ruled in his own favor. Daniel is summoned because he's the only living person who knew both men intimately — Newton's roommate at Cambridge, Leibniz's correspondent and philosophical sparring partner.

**The real stakes** — The dispute determined whether British or Continental mathematics would dominate European science. Newton's followers refused to adopt Leibniz's superior notation; British mathematics stagnated for over a century as a result. Daniel's mission is to prevent exactly this — though historically, he must fail.

**Hercules** — Enoch and Daniel discuss the mythological hero — the second time Enoch has brought up Hercules with a Waterhouse (the first is in *Cryptonomicon*). For two probable Deists to keep referencing pagan mythology is one of the novel's quiet jokes about how the Scientific Revolution didn't replace old stories so much as add new ones alongside them.
