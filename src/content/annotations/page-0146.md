Daniel contemplates the horizon and what lies beyond the visible world.

**"The western horizon"** — The original wiki annotation connects this to the broader theme of observation and the limits of human perception. In the 1660s, the boundary between what could and couldn't be seen was rapidly shifting — telescopes extending vision outward, microscopes extending it inward. The horizon is both a literal and metaphorical boundary in the novel: Daniel is poised between the old world of [Puritan](/topic/puritans) certainty and the new world of [natural philosophy](/topic/natural-philosophy), where truth must be observed rather than revealed.
