Aboard the *Minerva* off the coast of New England in November 1713, Daniel Waterhouse prepares for an Atlantic crossing while attempting to conduct scientific observations amidst a diverse crew.

**"drops out a Fahrenheit thermometer on a string. It is the very latest in temperature measurement technology"** — While [Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit](/topic/fahrenheit-thermometer) did not propose his standardized scale until 1724, he was actively developing these high-precision mercury thermometers in the years leading up to this voyage. This instrument represented a massive leap in accuracy for [Natural Philosophy](/topic/natural-philosophy), allowing for consistent data collection across different climates.

**"rumor that the North Atlantic is striped with currents of warm water"** — Mariners had long suspected the existence of the [Gulf Stream](/topic/gulf-stream), but it remained a mystery to science for decades. Stephenson's annotation: "The use of the thermometer as an instrument of navigation is credited to [Benjamin Franklin](/topic/ben-franklin) in 1775... who produced a reasonably accurate chart of the Gulf Stream."

**"atlantic is striped with currents"** — From the original wiki (Chris Swingley): "The existence of warm and cold bands of ocean water was first recorded in 1612 by Lescarbot: "I have found something remarkable upon which a natural philosopher should meditate." The use of the thermometer as an instrument of navigation is credited to Ben Franklin in 1775 and Charles Blagden in 1782. Franklin carried out rigorous ocean temperature experiments on many of his ocean voyages and produced a reasonably accurate chart of the Gulf Stream with his cousin and Nantucket whaling capitan Timothy Folger (1770)."

**"a Filipino; a Lascar; a half-African, half-white from the Portuguese city of Goa"** — This diverse roster illustrates the [Global Maritime Labor](/topic/lascars-and-global-seamen) market of the 18th century. "Lascars" was a broad term for sailors from South Asia or the Arab world who were essential to European merchant fleets.

**"a Huguenot"** — [Huguenots](/topic/huguenots) were French Protestants who fled their home country in waves following the 1685 Revocation of the Edict of Nantes. They became a significant diaspora in London and the American colonies, often bringing specialized artisan and banking skills with them.

**"fighting their way past pirates if need be"** — The early 1700s marked the height of the "Golden Age of [Piracy](/topic/pirates)," a period when state-sponsored privateering devolved into rampant lawlessness across Atlantic trade routes.

**"The page ends abruptly."** — This meta-textual break is a deliberate narrative device. Stephenson's annotation: "It's intended to be read continuously from the bottom of p. 92 to the top of 93, hence: 'all of a sudden it's [The Plague Year](/topic/the-plague-year).'"
