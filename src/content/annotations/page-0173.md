The coffeehouse — engine of the Enlightenment — takes center stage.

**"Coffee"** — Coffee arrived in England around 1650 and spread with astonishing speed. By 1663 there were 83 [coffeehouses](/topic/coffeehouses) in London; by 1700, over 500. They were called "penny universities" because for the price of a cup (one penny), you could sit all day, read newspapers, and join any conversation. Different coffeehouses attracted different crowds: merchants, scientists, writers, politicians. Lloyd's of London began as a coffeehouse where ship insurers gathered. The [Royal Society](/topic/royal-society)'s early meetings overlapped with coffeehouse culture. [Charles II](/topic/charles-ii) tried to suppress coffeehouses in 1675, calling them "seminaries of sedition," but backed down within eleven days after public outcry. Coffee itself was controversial — women's groups petitioned against it, claiming it made men impotent, while proponents argued it sharpened the mind and provided a sober alternative to the alehouses that had previously dominated English social life.
