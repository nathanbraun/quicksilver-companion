Leibniz is described as a "monster" — but in the 17th century, this word carried specific philosophical weight.

**"The monster"** — In early modern usage, a "monster" was anything that exceeded or violated natural laws — not necessarily fearful, but wondrous, beyond the ordinary. The term could be applied to prodigies, marvels, and exceptional human beings alike. The original wiki annotation connects this to [Leibniz](/topic/gottfried-wilhelm-von-leibniz) as a figure who exceeded normal human capacities. The word also has a mathematical meaning: the "Monster group," the largest sporadic simple group in mathematics, has an order of approximately 8 × 10^53. Richard Borcherds won the Fields Medal in 1998 for proving the "Monstrous Moonshine" conjecture connecting this group to modular functions — a result whose name captures the same sense of something that shouldn't exist but does, something that exceeds what the rules seem to allow.
