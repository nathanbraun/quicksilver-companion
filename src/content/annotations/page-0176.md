Restoration fashion and the visual culture of Charles II's court.

**"Black velvet"** — Restoration fashion was a dramatic break from [Puritan](/topic/puritans) austerity. [Charles II](/topic/charles-ii), raised at the French court, brought Continental extravagance to England. Men adopted the periwig (an enormous wig of false hair that could cost more than a servant's annual wages), while women wore low-cut gowns of silk and velvet. Patches — small shapes cut from black velvet or silk and applied to the face with glue — were both decorative and practical, used to hide smallpox scars. The fashion was self-consciously theatrical, reflecting the reopening of the [theaters](/topic/restoration-theater) and a court culture that valued display and wit. For Daniel, raised among [Puritans](/topic/puritans) who dressed in deliberate plainness, this world of velvet, lace, and artificial beauty marks was alien territory.
