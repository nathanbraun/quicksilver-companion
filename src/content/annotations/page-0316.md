Leibniz sketches a mathematical series that will echo across centuries and novels.

**"The first few terms of a series"** — The series [Leibniz](/topic/gottfried-wilhelm-von-leibniz) scratches in the dirt for Daniel is the Leibniz formula for π/4: 1 - 1/3 + 1/5 - 1/7 + 1/9 - ... This infinite series converges (very slowly) to π/4. It's one of the most beautiful results in mathematics — an infinite alternating sum of reciprocals of odd numbers producing a transcendental number. The original wiki annotator notes a deliberate Stephenson callback: in *Cryptonomicon*, Alan Turing shows this same series to Lawrence Waterhouse on page 15, "in a more compact (and modern) form." Two generations of [Waterhouses](/topic/daniel-waterhouse) educated in the Leibniz series — one by Leibniz himself, one by the man who formalized what Leibniz's dream could and couldn't achieve. The series connects the [priority dispute](/topic/calculus-priority-dispute) to the digital age.
