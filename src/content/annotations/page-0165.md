A philosophical puzzle about motion surfaces aboard the Minerva.

**"Zeno's Paradox"** — [Zeno's Paradox](/topic/zenos-paradox) is actually a series of paradoxes by the Greek philosopher Zeno of Elea (c. 495–430 BC), all attacking the coherence of motion and change. The one referenced here: to cross any distance, you must first cross half of it, then half of the remainder, and so on infinitely. Since you can never complete an infinite number of steps, motion is impossible. The argument is obviously wrong — but explaining *why* required mathematical tools that wouldn't exist until [Newton](/topic/isaac-newton) and [Leibniz](/topic/gottfried-wilhelm-von-leibniz) developed the calculus. The concept of a convergent infinite series — infinitely many terms summing to a finite value — is the key. That this ancient paradox connects directly to the [calculus priority dispute](/topic/calculus-priority-dispute) is exactly Stephenson's point.
