Enoch produces Princess Caroline's letter, and the Harvard mob reacts.

**White phosphorus** — The letter is "likened to" white phosphorus — volatile, self-igniting, impossible to ignore. The metaphor isn't random: phosphorus was discovered in 1669 by Hennig Brand, an alchemist boiling urine looking for the Philosopher's Stone. He found something that glowed in the dark and burst into flame. The real overlap between [alchemy](/topic/alchemy) and chemistry is one of the novel's central themes.

**[Queen Anne](/topic/queen-anne)** — The Harvard dons are "behaving as if Queen Anne were already dead and buried." She wasn't yet — but she was visibly dying, childless after seventeen pregnancies, and everyone was positioning for the succession. Anne was the last Stuart monarch, and her imminent death created a crisis that shapes every chapter set in 1713.

**[Princess Caroline of Ansbach](/topic/caroline-of-ansbach)** — "Their future Queen." The woman whose letter sets the entire plot in motion. Caroline was a genuine intellectual who sought out [Leibniz](/topic/gottfried-wilhelm-von-leibniz) as a philosophical mentor and corresponded with him seriously about metaphysics. She understood both the science and the politics of the [Newton](/topic/isaac-newton)-Leibniz dispute — arguably the only person in Europe who did.

**The [Hanoverian succession](/topic/hanoverian-succession)** — Why German-speaking Protestants are about to inherit the English throne. The Act of Settlement (1701) skipped over fifty-odd Catholics with stronger blood claims to designate Sophia of Hanover as Anne's heir. Leibniz is advisor to the Hanoverian court, which is why the [calculus](/topic/calculus-priority-dispute) dispute isn't just about math — it's entangled with the next dynasty's legitimacy.
