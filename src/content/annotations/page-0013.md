Enoch arrives in New York to find a slave revolt in progress.

**"Some Irish, the rest Angolan"** — The 1712 New York slave revolt was real. On the night of April 6, 1712, about two dozen enslaved Africans set fire to a building on Maiden Lane and attacked the whites who came to put it out, killing nine. The militia crushed the revolt by morning. The reprisals were savage: twenty-one were convicted, and the executions included burning at the stake, breaking on the wheel, and slow starvation in chains — punishments meant to terrorize. Six others killed themselves before trial. New York in 1712 was roughly 20% enslaved — the highest proportion of any English colonial city north of the Chesapeake.

**"Irish elements"** — The rowers on Daniel's pilot boat sing a dirge with Irish elements, a reminder that the line between indentured servitude and slavery was blurry in colonial America. A global famine starting in 1707 had pushed many Irish into bondage. Some were kidnapped outright — "Barbadosed" was the slang for being shipped to Caribbean plantations. Irish indentured servants and enslaved Africans sometimes worked side by side, though their legal statuses were different in theory if not always in practice.

**The Pennsylvania response** — In June 1712, the Pennsylvania Assembly banned slave importation — not from moral clarity, but from fear that New York's revolt would spread. The timing matters: Daniel is arriving into a colonial world that is both utterly dependent on slave labor and increasingly terrified of it.

**[Treaty of Utrecht](/topic/treaty-of-utrecht)** — Signed April 1713, just months before this chapter. The treaty ended the War of the Spanish Succession (the geopolitical backdrop of the entire novel). Among its terms: Britain gained control of the *asiento* — the monopoly contract to supply enslaved Africans to Spanish colonies in the Americas. Britain literally won the slave trade as a prize of war, and the South Sea Company was chartered to exploit it. The same treaty that shapes the novel's high politics is built on the traffic in human beings Daniel sees from the harbor.
