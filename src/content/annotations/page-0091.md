Daniel takes scientific measurements at sea.

**"The Atlantic is striped with currents"** — Daniel measures ocean temperature as the *Minerva* crosses the Atlantic, treating the voyage as a scientific expedition. The existence of warm and cold bands of ocean water was first recorded by Lescarbot in 1612. But the rigorous use of the thermometer for ocean navigation wouldn't come until Ben Franklin's experiments in 1775, when Franklin mapped the Gulf Stream with his Nantucket whaling-captain cousin Timothy Folger. Daniel, in 1713, is doing preliminary work that's decades ahead of standard practice — fitting for a Fellow of the [Royal Society](/topic/royal-society) who can't resist turning any experience into data. The [longitude problem](/topic/longitude) makes these measurements more than academic: if you can map ocean currents and temperature gradients, you have one more clue to your position at sea.
