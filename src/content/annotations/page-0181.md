Newton's alchemical connections lead Daniel into shadowy circles at court.

**"In the house of Monsieur Le Febure"** — Stephenson's annotation: "It is well attested that Newton devoted a lot of time to Alchemy and met frequently with fellow-Alchemists. The details are quite obscure; that is, there's really no telling whom he met with and when. Here I am building a story that is fictitious but not too implausible." Nicaise Le Febure (also Le Fèvre) was a French apothecary and chemist who served as Royal Professor of Chemistry to [Charles II](/topic/charles-ii). The king himself practiced [alchemy](/topic/alchemy), maintaining a private laboratory at Whitehall. Newton's alchemical pursuits are well documented — he wrote over a million words on the subject — but whom he collaborated with remains largely unknown, since alchemists prized secrecy. Stephenson uses this historical fog to place the young [Newton](/topic/isaac-newton) in London's alchemical underworld.
