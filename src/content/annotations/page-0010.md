Enoch examines a coin.

**"D.G. HISPAN ET IND REX"** — *Dei Gratia Hispaniae et Indiarum Rex*, "By the grace of God, King of Spain and the Indies." This is a **piece of eight** — an 8-reale silver coin, and the closest thing the early 18th century had to a world currency. Minted from Mexican and Peruvian silver, these coins circulated everywhere: Europe, the Americas, Asia, Africa. Chinese merchants accepted them. [Pirates](/topic/pirates) hoarded them.

Because they were pure silver, you could literally cut them into pieces without losing value. An eighth of a piece of eight was a "bit" — hence "two bits" for a quarter dollar. The US dollar was originally defined in terms of the Spanish dollar, and the "$" symbol may derive from the Pillars of Hercules stamped on the coin.

The nature of money — whether value lives in the metal or in the trust — is one of the central questions of the Baroque Cycle. This coin is the first move in that argument.
