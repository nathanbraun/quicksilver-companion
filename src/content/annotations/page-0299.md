The Arithmetickal Engine takes center stage at the Royal Society.

**"When you turn the crank?"** — [Leibniz](/topic/gottfried-wilhelm-von-leibniz) lived in Paris from 1672 to 1676 and made a trip to London in January 1673 to show his [calculating machine](/topic/computing-machines) prototype to the [Royal Society](/topic/royal-society). The Society's secretary, [Henry Oldenburg](/topic/henry-oldenburg) — a fellow German — facilitated the visit. The machine used "stepped drums" (cylinders with teeth of varying lengths) to perform multiplication and division mechanically, a significant advance over Pascal's 1642 calculator. Leibniz spent a fortune on the project throughout his life but never achieved fully automatic carrying. One of his models survives in the Hanover State Library.

**"Superior to man"** — Leibniz once considered commemorating his calculator with a medal bearing this motto. He later had a machine made for Peter the Great to send to the Emperor of China. The immediate applications were obvious: accounting, surveying, mathematical tables. But as the original wiki notes, even educated people in this era rarely understood multiplication — [Samuel Pepys](/topic/samuel-pepys) had to learn his multiplication tables while already a senior administrator at the Navy Office.

**"Let us calculate"** — Leibniz envisioned a larger version of the machine mechanizing *all* reasoning, once all possible thoughts had been given numbers through the [Universal Characteristic](/topic/universal-characteristic). Instead of arguing, people would compute. Jonathan Swift satirized this vision in *Gulliver's Travels* — the Academy of Lagado in the "Voyage to Balnibarbi" features a machine that generates random combinations of words to produce all possible books, a pointed parody of Leibnizian optimism about mechanical knowledge.
