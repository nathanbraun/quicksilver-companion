Jack and Eliza travel through a war-ravaged Bohemia toward the Rhine, reflecting on the history of the Thirty Years' War and their own pasts in London and the Caribbean.

**"Jack drew the Janissary’s sword out of the rolled blanket where it had been hid since Vienna"** — [Janissaries](/topic/janissaries) were the elite infantry of the Ottoman Sultan, originally composed of Christian youths converted to Islam and trained as a professional standing army.

**"since Vienna and washed the dried blood off in a creek-bend."** — The [Battle of Vienna](/topic/battle-of-vienna) in 1683 was a pivotal clash where a coalition of European forces broke a two-month Ottoman siege, halting the empire's expansion into Central Europe.

**"haiduks"** — From the original wiki (Professorbikeybike): "Serbs and Greeks never really accepted the Turkish invasion, and thus, those that chose to actively resist, were labeled haiduks, or outlaws. Haiduks were arguably not outlaws, as the law they broke were Turkish law, not their own. Haiduks would run ambushes on invading Turkish caravans, often with popular support from Serbian villagers.

It is interesting to note that George Washington Hayduke, the character in Edward Abbey's novel The Monkey Wrench Gang, most likely got his name from the haiduks."

**"Since the Papists slew all the decent folk, this is a country of bandits, haiduks, and Vagabonds"** — [Haiduks](/topic/haiduks) were Balkan outlaws or guerrilla fighters who resisted Ottoman rule; Stephenson's annotation notes that while labeled outlaws by the Turks, they often had popular support from local villagers.

**"For that is Damascus steel, more than likely."** — [Damascus steel](/topic/damascus-steel) is a legendary Middle Eastern sword-making material known for its "watered" pattern and exceptional sharpness; Stephenson attributes the accompanying poem in the text to the [Arabian Nights](/topic/arabian-nights) as translated by [Sir Richard Francis Burton](/topic/richard-francis-burton).

**"As the Grand Vizier recently learned, all the riches in the world are of no use if you can’t defend ’em."** — [Kara Mustafa Pasha](/topic/kara-mustafa-pasha) was the Ottoman Grand Vizier who led the failed siege of Vienna and was executed by strangulation with a silk cord for his defeat.

**"Two or three generations ago, ’twas a wheat-field... until something intervened—Religious controversy!"** — The [Thirty Years' War](/topic/thirty-years-war) (1618–1648) was one of the most destructive conflicts in European history, leaving Bohemia depopulated and its agriculture ruined for decades.

**"Ever seen a morality play?"** — A [morality play](/topic/morality-plays) is a medieval theatrical genre where personified abstractions (like Death or Virtue) struggle for a soul, a fitting metaphor for the grim landscape Jack and Eliza traverse.

**"it is like an Alchemist’s furnace in which a whole town was purified of heresy."** — [Alchemy](/topic/alchemy), the precursor to modern chemistry, sought to "purify" base metals into gold; here, Eliza uses it to describe the violent religious "purification" of the region.

**"One of those lords married an English girl, name of Elizabeth—the sister of Chuck the First."** — [Elizabeth Stuart](/topic/the-winter-queen), daughter of James I, became the Queen of Bohemia; her brief reign and numerous descendants are central to the dynastic politics of the *Baroque Cycle*.

**"Charles the First—isn’t he the one who ran afoul of Cromwell, and got his head chopped off"** — [Charles I](/topic/charles-i) was the English monarch executed in 1649 following his defeat by Parliamentary forces in the [English Civil War](/topic/english-civil-war).

**"threw several of ’em out a castle window into a dung-heap, and declared this country free of Popery."** — The [Defenestration of Prague](/topic/defenestration-of-prague) in 1618, where Protestant nobles tossed Catholic regents out of a window, served as the immediate spark for the Thirty Years' War.

**"The Winter Queen and the Winter King, as they were called after that"** — [Frederick V and Elizabeth Stuart](/topic/winter-king-and-queen) earned these nicknames because their reign in Bohemia lasted only a single winter before they were forced into exile.

**"Port Royal. I wanted to see Port Royal, which is to pirates as Amsterdam is to Jews."** — [Port Royal](/topic/port-royal) in Jamaica was the premier base for 17th-century privateers and was known as the "wickedest city on Earth" before an earthquake destroyed it in 1692.

**"We used pieces of eight in Constantinople."** — [Pieces of eight](/topic/piece-of-eight) were Spanish silver pesos that functioned as the world's first truly global currency, accepted from the Americas to the Ottoman Empire.

**"only a few years since Captain Morgan had personally sacked Portobelo and Panama"** — [Henry Morgan](/topic/henry-morgan) was a Welsh privateer whose raids on Spanish strongholds made him a legend and eventually the Lieutenant Governor of Jamaica.

**"I know that in the Cape Verde Islands and Madeira, all sugar is cultivated by slaves"** — The 17th-century [sugar trade](/topic/sugar-and-slavery) was the engine of the Atlantic economy, built entirely on the brutal system of enslaved African labor.

**"Jack explained airily... No one but a Frenchman wears those sabots."** — [Sabots](/topic/sabots) are traditional wooden shoes; Stephenson notes the popular (though historically debated) theory that the word "sabotage" comes from workers throwing these shoes into machinery.

**"useless weight to him since the destruction of Brown Bess"** — [Brown Bess](/topic/brown-bess) is the traditional nickname for the British Land Pattern Musket, though the term did not become common parlance until the mid-18th century.

**"The great hangings at Tyburn stopped."** — [Tyburn](/topic/tyburn-tree) was London's primary site for public executions, featuring a unique triangular gallows that could hang twenty-four people at once.

**"frenchman wears those sabots"** — From the original wiki (Professorbikeybike): "Note: this isn't the first appearence of the term sabot.

A bit of etymology:

While a sabot is merely a wooden shoe, to sabotage something is quite different. "The word is said to date from a French railway strike of 1910 when workers destroyed the wooden shoes (sabots) that held the rails in place" (http://www.britannica.com/eb/article?eu=66308&tocid=0&query=sabotage&ct=).
The story that the word sabotage originally referred to damage done to machinery by sabots is not believed to be true.

Another note: N."

**"Theatres: shut down for the first time since Cromwell."** — [Oliver Cromwell](/topic/oliver-cromwell) and the Puritans banned theater during the Interregnum, viewing it as sinful; the theaters were closed again during the 1665 plague to prevent the spread of disease.

**"Ranters, Diggers, Levellers, Quakers"** — These [radical religious groups](/topic/radical-religious-groups) emerged during the English Civil War, often advocating for the abolition of private property or the equality of all believers.

**"Now, a year later London burnt to the ground"** — The [Great Fire of London](/topic/great-fire-of-london) in 1666 destroyed the medieval city center, including 13,000 houses and 87 parish churches.

**"undoing Enclosure, poached game in forests"** — [Enclosure](/topic/enclosure-acts) was the legal process of fencing off common lands for private use, a practice that impoverished many English peasants and forced them into vagrancy.

**"as if she were an actress on a Southwark stage"** — [Restoration theater](/topic/restoration-theater) was a cultural explosion following the return of the monarchy in 1660, notable for being the first time women were legally allowed to perform on the English stage.

**"When Polish serfs run away, their lords hunt ’em down and brand ’em"** — While [serfdom](/topic/serfdom) was fading in Western Europe, it intensified in Eastern Europe during the 17th century, binding peasants to the land with increasing severity.

**"The Devil’s Poor"** — In the early modern era, the [deserving and undeserving poor](/topic/deserving-and-undeserving-poor) were distinguished by their ability to work; "The Devil's Poor" were those seen as choosing a life of crime or idleness.

**"Sir Winston Churchill"** — Stephenson's annotation notes that this [Sir Winston Churchill](/topic/sir-winston-churchill-cavalier) was a real 17th-century Royalist and historian, the father of the Duke of Marlborough and ancestor of the famous Prime Minister.

**"Clubmen—armies of peasants who’d once roamed that part of the country with cudgels"** — [Clubmen](/topic/clubmen) were local defense militias during the English Civil War who attempted to protect their homes from being plundered by both the Royalist and Parliamentary armies.

**"Lord Lieutenant thereabouts—that’s a job that the King gives to the gents"** — A [Lord Lieutenant](/topic/lord-lieutenant) was the monarch's direct representative in an English county, tasked with overseeing the local militia and maintaining the peace.

**"The countryside was divided up into innumerable separate pays with their own tolls"** — Pre-Revolutionary France was a patchwork of [internal customs barriers](/topic/french-internal-customs) (pays) that made transporting goods across the country incredibly expensive and slow.

**"young John Churchill"** — [John Churchill](/topic/john-churchill), later the 1st Duke of Marlborough, would become one of the most successful military commanders in British history during the upcoming wars against Louis XIV.
