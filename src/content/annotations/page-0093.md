London, 1665. The [Plague](/topic/plague) arrives.

**The Plague Year** — The Great Plague of 1665 killed an estimated 100,000 Londoners — roughly a quarter of the city's population. It was the last major outbreak of bubonic plague in England, caused by *Yersinia pestis* bacteria carried by fleas on black rats. Symptoms appeared 2-6 days after a flea bite: fever, chills, then the horrifying buboes — swollen lymph nodes the size of eggs, usually in the groin or armpits. Subcutaneous hemorrhaging turned the skin purple-black, hence "Black Death." Mortality without treatment: 50-90%.

The city's response was quarantine (from the Italian *quaranta giorni*, forty days — the period Venice required ships to wait before disembarking). Houses with plague victims were sealed shut, with the healthy locked inside alongside the dying. The wealthy fled to the countryside. The poor died in the streets. Daniel Defoe's *A Journal of the Plague Year* (1722) remains the most vivid account, though written from research rather than memory.

For the novel, the plague is pivotal: it empties Cambridge, sending [Newton](/topic/isaac-newton) home to Woolsthorpe where he will invent [calculus](/topic/calculus-priority-dispute), discover the spectrum of light, and begin work on gravity — his *annus mirabilis*. Catastrophe creates genius.
