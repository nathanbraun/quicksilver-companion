The novel opens on Boston Common: a hanging, a mysterious older man, a young boy. October 12, 1713, 10:33:52 A.M.

**Jack Ketch** — The executioner is called "Jack Ketch," which had become generic slang for any hangman. The real Jack Ketch was London's notorious Tyburn executioner, famous for botching beheadings. Stephenson noted in his own annotation that the name traces to Richard Jaquett, who held the manor of Tyburn, and that using it in Boston is "somewhat anachronistic" — but it works.

**Lobsterbacks** — Slang for British redcoats, more commonly associated with the Revolution sixty years later. Their presence here reminds us that colonial Boston, for all its [Puritan](/topic/puritans) self-governance, was under Crown military watch. Boston Common served triple duty: grazing pasture, militia training ground, execution site.

**"Dollop of hills"** — More literal than it sounds. Stephenson's own annotation: Boston in 1713 had a "completely different shape" from today. It was nearly an island — a bulbous peninsula connected by a narrow strip called the Neck, dominated by three hills (the "Trimountain," hence Tremont Street). Modern Boston is mostly built on landfill that was underwater in 1713. He recommends Whitehill's *Boston, a Topographical History*.

**The timestamp** — How does anyone in 1713 know the time to the second? A small puzzle. On land, precise time could come from astronomical observation (Galileo's method of timing Jupiter's moons had been in use since ~1650). At sea, the problem was unsolved — the [longitude question](/topic/longitude) that haunts the whole Baroque Cycle. The date is also October 12 — Columbus Day.
