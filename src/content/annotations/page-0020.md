Godfrey Waterhouse asks Enoch how he can possibly be as old as his stories imply.

**"Ask your father"** — Enoch deflects. He won't explain his impossible longevity, not here, not anywhere in the novel. This is one of the book's sustained mysteries: Enoch appears in *Cryptonomicon* in the 1940s and 1990s, apparently unaged. The biblical Enoch "walked with God" and was taken up without dying (Genesis 5:24). Whether Root found the [Philosopher's Stone](/topic/alchemy), is a time traveler, or is something else entirely, Stephenson never says.

**"A harder question"** — Jeremy Bornstein's original wiki annotation: "It seems as if this cryptic comment by Enoch Root must be a clue as to his true nature. This comment makes it sound like he's a time traveller to me, although other passages just seem to indicate that he has just lived an extraordinarily long time." The ambiguity is the point. Stephenson gives you enough to speculate, never enough to resolve.

**"Most disorderly"** — Enoch says his memory "lacks tidiness." If he's lived centuries, or experienced time non-linearly, the mess makes sense. A human brain wasn't designed to hold three hundred years of continuous experience.
