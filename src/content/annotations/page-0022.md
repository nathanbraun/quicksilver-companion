Enoch describes the coronation of Louis XIV and dismisses alchemy.

**Angelbalm, a thousand years old** — [Louis XIV](/topic/louis-xiv)'s coronation in 1654 used the Oil of Clovis, legendarily delivered by a dove to the bishop who baptized the first Frankish king in 496 AD. Every pre-Revolutionary French king was anointed with it. Ben's reaction — "it must have stunk to high heaven!" — gets one of Enoch's best lines: "Hard to say, in France."

**"T'was all rubbish"** — Enoch's verdict on [alchemy](/topic/alchemy). Striking because Enoch himself seems to have achieved what alchemists spent centuries pursuing — eternal life. Either he's lying, or he found the answer through some other means, or he genuinely tried alchemy and moved on. Stephenson lets all three possibilities coexist.

**[John Wilkins](/topic/john-wilkins)... Cryptonomicon** — Stephenson's own annotation: Wilkins published *Mercury* in 1641, "the first English-language work on cryptology" per David Kahn's *The Codebreakers*. In Stephenson's alternate universe, the book is called *Cryptonomicon* — the same title as his earlier novel. "And just to make things a little more confusing, in the real world is an Internet document called *Cyphernomicon*, by Tim May, which predates my work by many years!"

**Monsieur Le Febure** — Stephenson's own annotation: "Le Febure was for real, but I don't know much about him, so most of what appears concerning him in this book is made up." Refreshing authorial honesty.
