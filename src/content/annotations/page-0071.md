Newton's self-experimentation reaches alarming levels.

**Eyeball experiments** — Newton sticks a bodkin (a blunt needle) between his eye and the bone of his eye socket to study how pressure distorts vision. This is not Stephenson's invention. Newton's own notebook records: "I tooke a bodkine gh & put it betwixt my eye & [the] bone as neare to [the] backside of my eye as I could." He drew a diagram of the procedure. He also stared at the sun until he nearly went blind, then sat in a dark room for days cataloguing the afterimages. These experiments were dangerous and painful but not irrational — Newton was trying to determine whether light was a property of the external world or a product of the eye itself. The answer (both, in different ways) would take centuries to fully work out. Daniel's role as horrified witness to Newton's self-mutilation is one of the novel's recurring set pieces.
