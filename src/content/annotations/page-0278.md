Leibniz reveals his grandest ambition: a symbolic calculus that could mechanically determine truth.

**"Truth can be grasped as if pictured on paper"** — This is the heart of Leibniz's [Universal Characteristic](/topic/universal-characteristic): the *calculus ratiocinator*, a system for reducing all reasoning to symbolic manipulation. Assign numbers to basic concepts, define logical operations as arithmetic, and a [computing machine](/topic/computing-machines) could churn out truths mechanically. [Leibniz](/topic/gottfried-wilhelm-von-leibniz) got as far as the principles of non-contradiction (a statement can't be both true and false) and the identity of indiscernibles (two things with identical properties are the same thing). He envisioned philosophers resolving disputes by saying "Let us calculate!" — and sitting down at a machine to do so. In 1931, Kurt Gödel proved this dream impossible: any formal system powerful enough to express basic arithmetic will contain true statements it cannot prove. The impossibility was itself proven by a technique — Gödel numbering — strikingly similar to Leibniz's own method of encoding concepts as products of primes.
