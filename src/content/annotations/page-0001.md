The epigraph, before the story begins. A quote from Roger Cotes's preface to the second edition of [Newton](/topic/isaac-newton)'s *Principia* (1713): "Those who assume hypotheses as first principles of their speculations... may indeed form an ingenious romance, but a romance it will still be."

**Roger Cotes** — A young Cambridge mathematician who edited the *Principia*'s second edition. This preface was a shot at [Leibniz](/topic/gottfried-wilhelm-von-leibniz) in the [priority dispute](/topic/calculus-priority-dispute). Cotes is dismissing anyone who speculates about *why* gravity works (as Leibniz did) rather than just describing *how* (as Newton insisted with his famous *hypotheses non fingo* — "I do not feign hypotheses").

**The double meaning** — Per Stephenson's own annotation: "romance" was a synonym for "novel." So Cotes's insult accidentally describes the book you're holding. Stephenson: "This romance assumes hypotheses as a first principle, i.e. it is based on some fictitious imaginings of the author. So it is not a history, but merely a 'romance' which I hope at least some readers will find 'ingenious.'" He also noted that his wiki annotations exist "to assist those who are curious in teasing apart what is purely fictitious from what is based in historical evidence." This companion carries on that spirit.
