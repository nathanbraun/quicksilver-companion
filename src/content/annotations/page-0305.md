The Minerva narrative turns to the mathematical dynasty that championed Leibniz's calculus.

**"The Bernoullis"** — The [Bernoulli family](/topic/bernoulli-family) of Basel produced at least eight prominent mathematicians across three generations. Jakob (1655–1705) and Johann (1667–1748) were among the first to master [Leibniz](/topic/gottfried-wilhelm-von-leibniz)'s differential calculus and apply it to real problems — fluid dynamics, probability, the shape of hanging chains. Johann became Leibniz's most aggressive champion in the [priority dispute](/topic/calculus-priority-dispute) with [Newton](/topic/isaac-newton), publicly mocking Newton's notation as inferior. The brothers' own rivalry was legendary: they publicly challenged each other with mathematical problems and accused each other of plagiarism. Johann's son Daniel (1700–1783) would discover the principle explaining how airplane wings generate lift — which Johann then plagiarized by backdating his own rival publication.
