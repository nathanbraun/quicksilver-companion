Newton at Woolsthorpe — the famous plague retreat where modern physics was born.

**"Newton at Woolsthorpe"** — Woolsthorpe Manor, Newton's family home in Lincolnshire, is where he retreated when Cambridge closed during the [plague](/topic/plague). Stephenson's annotation: "It is well attested that [Newton](/topic/isaac-newton) devoted a lot of time to [Alchemy](/topic/alchemy) and met frequently with fellow-Alchemists." Newton later described 1665–1666 as his *annus mirabilis*, claiming he developed the foundations of [calculus](/topic/calculus-priority-dispute), the theory of colors, and the inverse-square law of gravitation during this period. Modern historians are skeptical — Newton likely exaggerated in retrospect to bolster his priority claims against [Leibniz](/topic/gottfried-wilhelm-von-leibniz). But the Woolsthorpe period was genuinely productive, and Stephenson places Daniel there as witness, giving the reader a front-row seat to the birth of Newtonian science alongside [Newton's optical experiments](/topic/newtons-optics).
