Newton's intensity and the mathematical world's unfinished business come into focus.

**"Punishments"** — Stephenson's annotation discusses the punishments associated with academic and scientific disputes of the era. The world of [natural philosophy](/topic/natural-philosophy) in the 1660s was not yet professionalized — there were no peer-reviewed journals, no tenure committees, no established protocols for credit. Disputes over priority were personal, bitter, and sometimes violent.

**"Word arrived that Fermat had died"** — Pierre de Fermat died on January 12, 1665. His famous "Last Theorem" — the marginal note claiming he had a proof that x^n + y^n = z^n has no integer solutions for n > 2, but that the margin was too small to contain it — was published posthumously by his son in 1670. The theorem remained unproven for 328 years until Andrew Wiles's proof in 1995. Fermat's death during the [plague](/topic/plague) years is a reminder that the mathematical community of the 1660s was tiny and fragile; the loss of a single mind could leave problems unsolved for centuries. See [Fermat's Last Theorem](/topic/fermats-last-theorem).
